Slide 3 · Inline Control Tags~50s

Granular Control via Inline Tags

So what does it actually feel like to use?

Higgs v3 gives you granular control over the generated speech through inline tags, right inside the text. You write <|emotion:elation|> and the voice lights up. You write <|style:whispering|> mid-sentence, and it drops to a whisper — same speaker, same sentence. <|prosody:speed_slow|> slows it down.

There are over twenty emotions, styles like singing and shouting, prosody controls for speed, pitch and pauses, even sound events like laughter and sighs. They compose, they switch seamlessly mid-sentence — and this works across more than 110 languages.

Slide 6 · The TTS Landscape~70s

Positioning Higgs 4B

Let me position Higgs in the landscape, because TTS in 2026 is crowded.

On the scale axis, models run from Kokoro at 82 million parameters up to MOSS-1.5 at 8 billion. Higgs v3 sits at 4B — deliberately. Big enough for expressiveness across 110 languages; small enough to serve fast on a single GPU.

On the architecture axis there are four families. Mel-based models — the FastSpeech, VITS generation. Pure flow and diffusion models like F5-TTS. Then the LM-based families: AR language model plus a flow or diffusion decoder — that's CosyVoice, VibeVoice — and AR language model plus a discrete codec — VALL-E, Fish Speech, and Higgs.

We bet on AR + codec, and the reason is systems-shaped: when speech is just discrete tokens from a language model, every trick the LLM serving world has built becomes available to TTS. Keep that thought — it's the whole second half of this talk.

Slide 7 · Codec Architecture~55s

The Higgs Codec Tokenizer

The first half of the bet: the codec. The Higgs Codec Tokenizer turns waveforms into tokens and back, with about 200 million parameters.

It's a dual-path design. A semantic encoder — Wav2Vec — captures what is being said, supervised against a frozen teacher with a semantic loss. An acoustic encoder captures how it sounds. The two paths are concatenated and quantized by an eight-layer residual vector quantizer — RVQ — which gives us eight codebooks per frame, at 25 frames per second. Decoders on the other side reconstruct the waveform with both semantic and acoustic losses.

So every audio frame becomes eight small integers that carry both meaning and texture. That's what the language model learns to predict.

Slide 8 · The AR Model~55s

The Autoregressive TTS Model

The second half: the AR model itself. It's a roughly 4-billion-parameter decoder — 36 layers, hidden size 2560, grouped-query attention 32-to-8 — consuming interleaved text and audio tokens.

A few engineering details worth calling out. The eight codebook embeddings are fused into a single tensor, tied with the text embedding — one lookup, one head, instead of eight. Context is 8K tokens, which matters because generation is multi-turn: reference text, reference audio, then alternating text and audio chunks, each new chunk conditioned on everything before it.

That's what makes the cloning zero-shot and the streaming conversational. Output is 24 kHz audio at 25 fps — 40 milliseconds per frame.

Slide 9 · The Delay Pattern~55s

Delay Pattern: Accelerate Token Prediction

Now, eight codebooks per frame creates a sequencing problem, and the delay pattern is the elegant fix.

The naive option is to flatten: predict all eight tokens of frame one, then all eight of frame two. Your sequence is now eight times longer — eight times the decode steps. Unacceptable.

The delay pattern instead offsets each codebook diagonally: codebook k starts k steps after codebook zero. After a seven-step ramp, every single step emits eight tokens in parallel — one per codebook — and the sequence stays T plus seven instead of T times eight. The coarse codebook leads, carrying pitch and intonation; the fine ones trail, adding texture.

One step per audio frame. That's the acceleration.

Slide 10 · Evaluation~45s

How Do We Know It's Good?

Two axes.

First, fidelity of zero-shot voice cloning, on the industry-standard suites — SeedTTS, CV3-eval, Minimax-eval, and our own Higgs-multilingual-eval covering 111 languages and dialects. We hold single-digit word and character error rates across the board.

Second — and this is where most TTS models fall apart — the hard, interactive stuff. Emergent TTS-eval covers emotion, paralinguistics, foreign words mid-sentence, questions, tricky syntax, complex pronunciation.

If you want a TTS model for real conversations rather than audiobook narration, this second column is the one to look at.

Slide 11 · LLM vs TTS~55s

TTS Inference Is Not LLM Inference

OK — inference. This is where I want to spend the rest of our time, because the punchline is: TTS inference is not LLM inference, even when there's an LLM inside.

LLM inference is one dominant decode loop. One model, one scheduler, text in, text out. The token you sample is the product — you flush it to the user and you're done.

TTS inference is a multi-stage audio-generation pipeline: preprocess, then a reference-audio encoder, then the AR engine, then a vocoder. The token you sample is just an intermediate — it's worthless until a second GPU model turns it into a waveform. The stages have different compute patterns, different latency budgets, and you're balancing real-time latency against audio quality, not just maximizing throughput.

Slide 12 · SGLang-Omni Overview~60s

Declare Stages, Choose Schedulers

So we built the serving layer on SGLang-Omni, and the design philosophy fits on one line: declare stages, choose schedulers, and let the runtime handle routing, streaming, and resource isolation.

A Coordinator routes requests across the four stages. Each stage picks the scheduler that fits its job: preprocessing gets a CPU-side thread pool; the encoder gets a lean single-threaded scheduler; the AR engine runs the full OmniScheduler — which inherits SGLang's continuous batching, radix cache, and CUDA graph support; and the vocoder gets a streaming-aware scheduler. Underneath, a ZMQ control plane and a relay data plane move tensors over shared memory, NCCL, or CUDA IPC, and every stage declares its own GPU memory budget up front.

One detail I love: PR 476 deleted our bespoke HiggsScheduler and replaced it with the shared one. Less custom code, more inherited optimization.

Slide 13 · Disaggregated Stages~40s

Same Request = Ordered Stages

Why stages? This slide is the answer.

If one worker owns the whole pipeline end to end, request two waits for all of request one — even when the encoder is free and only the AR engine is busy. Disaggregate the stages into independent queues, and each request still flows in order — preprocess, encode, generate, vocode — but different requests overlap freely: while request one is in the vocoder, request two is mid-decode and request three is being encoded.

Same per-request order, less blocking, better scheduling, higher utilization. One slow stage stops blocking everything.

Slide 14 · Preprocessing~35s

Stage 1: Preprocessing

Now one slide per stage, in pipeline order.

Preprocessing parses the request, normalizes text and inline control tokens, validates the reference audio. Almost zero FLOPs — it's pure CPU and I/O work. The insight is that its real cost is boundary overhead, not compute.

So we run it on a CPU thread pool that preps several requests in parallel, and we let the encoder path absorb nearby preprocessing work to cut a stage boundary. The GPU never waits on I/O — by the time it finishes one request, the next is already prepped.

Slide 15 · The Encoder~50s

Stage 2: The Reference-Audio Encoder

The reference-audio encoder converts your voice sample into conditioning tokens. It costs 50 to 100 milliseconds — and here's the profile finding: it's deterministic and it's repetitive. Users reuse the same handful of voices across thousands of prompts. Most encoder work is recomputing answers we already know.

So: an LRU cache keyed by the reference audio — repeat voices skip the encoder entirely. Batched encoding for utilization — though we learned to keep that for offline warmup, because online batching caused GPU contention with the latency-critical decode loops. And torch.compile, which is a genuinely good fit here — static shapes, convolutional stack, no branching.

Slide 16 · The AR Engine~100s — the core slide

Stage 3: CUDA Graphs & Async Decode

The AR engine is the dominant stage, so this is where the heavy machinery goes. Ten seconds of speech is 400 to 800 decode steps, and each step's overhead multiplies by that count. A tenth of a millisecond per step is 80 milliseconds per request, gone.

Two foundations come free from reusing the shared scheduler: paged attention, which lets sixteen concurrent decode loops share one H100 without fragmenting KV memory, and the radix cache, namespaced per reference voice, so repeated voices skip the prompt prefill — it stacks with the encoder's LRU.

The biggest lever is CUDA Graphs. Without them, the CPU launches every small kernel one by one — each launch costs 5 to 10 microseconds, and the GPU starves between launches. A CUDA graph captures the whole decode step once and replays it with a single cheap launch. The catch: the capture is frozen — no data-dependent branching — so we rewrote the delay-pattern state machine as branchless, in-place tensor operations on fixed buffers. Control flow became data flow.

And then one more bubble: every step ends with a heavy host-side collect — sampler-state scatter, delay-pattern bookkeeping, end-of-stream handling — in Python, every step, with the GPU idle through all of it. The fix is async decode: a launch-first, one-step lookahead. We split the step into a launch half — forward, on-GPU sampling, an async copy into a pinned ping-pong buffer — and a resolve half, the host collect. The loop launches step N, then resolves step N−1, so the previous step's collect hides under the current forward.

Output is bit-identical with it on or off — and on the full benchmark at concurrency sixteen it's worth another 12.7% throughput, 16% off mean latency, and the tail RTF, p99, drops by almost 40 percent. The longer the request, the more it pays.

Slide 17 · The Vocoder~60s

Stage 4: Streaming Without the Clicks

Last stage: the vocoder, codebook tokens to 24-kilohertz waveform. Fast per call — about ten milliseconds — but it has two traps.

Trap one: queueing. Every decode loop converges here, so under load it becomes a tail-latency source. Fix: batched vocoder decode — collect requests in a two-millisecond window, decode as one batch.

Trap two: streaming. You cannot chunk audio the way you chunk text — the codec carries convolutional state across time, and naive chunk boundaries produce an audible click at every seam. So the streaming path decodes in overlapping windows: accumulate codes to a stride, backtrack into the previous window and re-decode jointly, crossfade the overlap, and hold back the final frames whose codebooks haven't filled yet.

Result: real SSE streaming, no clicks — first audio in roughly 350 milliseconds, well under the conversational threshold.

Slide 18 · Performance~45s

What Does All of That Buy?

One H100, full Seed-TTS English set.

At concurrency one: 617 milliseconds mean latency, real-time factor 0.147 — that's seven times faster than real time. Scale to concurrency sixteen: 14.7 requests per second, generating almost 62 seconds of audio every second, and the real-time factor is still only 0.26. Sixteen times the load, latency grows less than two-x. That's the multi-stage async design absorbing load.

And the guardrail throughout the whole optimization roadmap was: no model performance regression. Speed was never traded for WER.

Anticipated Q&A

Why 4B and not bigger?

The latency budget. Conversational TTS needs sub-500ms first audio; 4B holds single-digit WER across 110+ languages while serving at RTF 0.147 on a single H100. Bigger buys little quality, costs real latency.

Why AR + codec over flow/diffusion?

Serving leverage: discrete tokens inherit the entire LLM inference stack — continuous batching, radix cache, CUDA graphs. Flow heads need a separate sampling stage that's harder to batch and stream.

Doesn't the delay pattern hurt quality vs flattening?

The coarse-to-fine offset preserves the conditioning structure — codebook k sees codebook k−1's current frame. Benchmarks show no quality regression, and decoding is ~8× shorter.

How much reference audio for cloning?

A few seconds. More helps prosody matching; cache + radix make repeated voices nearly free.

Is async decode safe — doesn't lookahead change outputs?

No — only the host collect is deferred one step; the token causal chain stays on the GPU. Outputs verified bit-identical ON vs OFF (greedy, bs=1 and bs=4, 100/100), and bs=1 takes a sync fast path since there's nothing to overlap.

What about voice-cloning misuse?

API-level controls and usage policies; the open release ships with responsible-use terms — happy to discuss offline.
